How the Money Moves

By SCOTT GEIER

In the United States, geographic mobility has long been tied to economic mobility; people move out to move up, to get better jobs and make more money. But what about people who already make a good living? FiveThirtyEight analyzed census data to find out about the migratory patterns of people earning $75,000 or more.

  • Methodology

  • The following visualizations were created using data from the U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey (ACS), an annual survey that the government uses to determine the distribution of federal and state funds. In regard to domestic migration, the ACS tracks the annual number of people who live in the same residence as the previous year, those moved within their county of residence, those who moved to a new county in the same state, and those who left for a different state.

The Restless West

This map shows the percentage of people earning $75,000 or more who relocated in 2014. And it shows how they moved -- from house to house in the same county, to a new county in the same state, or out of the state entirely. Click the categories below to learn more.

The movement of high-income earners within counties reflects the dominance of a handful of cities. Take Nevada for example: if people are going to move in that state, they are most likely to move from one house to another in Clark County, the location of Las Vegas. The same is true for Arizona (Phoenix), Washington (Seattle) and California (San Francisco and Los Angeles).

This map seems to mark the cities that have multiple affluent counties within their metropolitan areas. The greater Atlanta area, for example, features five of the wealthiest counties in the state. Well-off Georgia residents are most likely to move from one part of Atlanta to another.

The interstate migration of high-income earners bucks the overall trend. Since the 1960s, the American population has been steadily moving from the Northeast and Midwest to the Sun Belt, the swath of warm-weather states that stretches from California to Florida. But for people earning more than $75,000, the opposite may be true; several Sun Belt states, like South Carolina, Tennessee, New Mexico and Arizona, actually lost the greatest numbers of residents.

Stay Put and Weather the Storm

The dip in this line graph is the shape of the Great Recession. High-income individuals moved less each year from 2006 (just before the start of the Recession) to 2009 (when it bottomed out). As property values plummeted, it became harder for people to relocate without taking losses on their homes.

You're Not From Here, Are You?

A majority of Americans (59 percent) live in the same state where they were born, according to the most recent Census data. But in the wealthiest parts of the country, signficantly fewer people on average (51 percent) are natives.

This table shows the list of the 50 wealthiest counties in the United States. The Washington, D.C., metropolitan area dominates the list, and the number of people who are from the area -- people who hail from Virginia and Maryland -- is incredibly low. For example, only 19 percent of the residents of Arlington County, Virginia, are actually from Virginia.

There are also a few oddities and outliers in the list. Explore the orange counties in the table to see more.
